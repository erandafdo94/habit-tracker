Bad habits don't disappear through willpower. Trying to stop a behaviour by sheer force of will works temporarily, but the underlying loop remains. Remove the willpower, and the habit returns.

The more effective approach is replacement: understand what the habit is doing for you, and give your brain a different way to get the same thing.

## The habit loop

Every habit runs on a three-part loop:

1. **Cue** — a trigger that initiates the behaviour
2. **Routine** — the behaviour itself
3. **Reward** — the payoff that reinforces the loop

Bad habits persist because they deliver real rewards. Scrolling social media delivers novelty and social connection. Snacking delivers a brief mood lift. Smoking delivers stress relief and a break from work.

You can't eliminate the reward without creating a gap. The brain will fill that gap with the original behaviour.

## Step 1: Identify the actual reward

Most people misidentify the reward of their bad habits. They think they smoke because they're addicted to nicotine. But many smokers report that what they actually crave is the break — stepping outside, pausing work, having a moment alone.

Charles Duhigg's research on habit loops suggests a diagnostic approach: when you feel the urge to perform the bad habit, pause and ask what you're actually craving. Is it stimulation? Stress relief? Social connection? A break? Comfort?

The answer tells you what the replacement needs to deliver.

## Step 2: Identify the cue

Cues fall into five categories:
- **Time** (3pm slump → snacking)
- **Location** (sitting on the sofa → watching TV)
- **Emotional state** (stressed → scrolling)
- **Other people** (certain friends → drinking more)
- **Preceding action** (finishing a task → checking phone)

Track the bad habit for a week. Each time it happens, note the time, location, emotional state, who you're with, and what you just did. Patterns will emerge.

## Step 3: Design a replacement

The replacement needs to:
- Be triggered by the same cue
- Deliver the same (or similar) reward
- Be easier to do than the bad habit, at least initially

Examples:
- **Cue:** 3pm energy dip. **Bad habit:** sugary snack. **Reward:** energy boost. **Replacement:** 5-minute walk outside.
- **Cue:** stress at work. **Bad habit:** scrolling social media. **Reward:** mental break. **Replacement:** 3 minutes of slow breathing or stepping outside.
- **Cue:** sitting on the sofa after dinner. **Bad habit:** two hours of passive TV. **Reward:** relaxation. **Replacement:** one episode with a specific end time, then read.

The replacement doesn't need to be virtuous. It just needs to deliver the reward more effectively than the bad habit, with fewer downsides.

## Step 4: Make the bad habit harder

Reduce the automaticity of the bad habit by adding friction:
- Delete social media apps from your phone (reinstalling takes effort)
- Don't keep snack foods in the house
- Put your phone in another room at night
- Log out of streaming services so opening them requires a deliberate action

Friction doesn't eliminate the habit, but it breaks the automatic loop. The pause created by friction gives you a moment to choose the replacement instead.

## Step 5: Track the replacement, not the elimination

Tracking "days without X" focuses attention on the bad habit. Tracking "days I did Y instead" focuses attention on the replacement.

The goal is not to stop doing something — it's to build a new loop. Track the new behaviour. The old one fades as the new one strengthens.

## What doesn't work

**Cold turkey for deeply ingrained habits.** For habits with strong physiological components (smoking, alcohol, certain foods), abrupt elimination creates intense cravings that are hard to sustain. Gradual replacement is more durable.

**Relying on motivation.** Motivation fluctuates. The replacement habit needs to be easy enough to do on low-motivation days.

**Punishing yourself for slipping.** Guilt and self-criticism are not effective behaviour change tools. They increase stress, which often triggers the bad habit. Note the slip, identify what triggered it, adjust the system, and move on.

---

Breaking a bad habit is not about willpower or discipline. It's about understanding the loop and engineering a better one. The bad habit is solving a problem — your job is to find a better solution to the same problem.
