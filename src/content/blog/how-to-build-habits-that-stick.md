Most people try to build habits through willpower. That's why most habits fail.

Willpower is a finite resource. It depletes throughout the day. Relying on it to sustain a new behaviour is like trying to drive across the country on a quarter tank of fuel.

The research on habit formation points to a different approach: make the behaviour automatic by attaching it to existing routines, reducing friction, and tracking consistently.

## Why habits fail

There are three common failure modes:

1. **Too ambitious too fast.** Starting with 30 minutes of exercise when you haven't moved in months. The bar is too high, so you skip it, feel bad, and quit.
2. **No trigger.** A habit without a cue never gets started. "I'll meditate sometime today" rarely happens.
3. **No feedback loop.** Without tracking, you don't notice progress. Without progress, motivation dies.

## The three-part system

### 1. Start smaller than feels useful

If you want to read more, start with one page a night. If you want to exercise, start with five minutes. The goal in week one is not results — it's showing up.

Once showing up is automatic, you can increase the intensity. But you can't increase what you never start.

### 2. Stack it onto something existing

Habit stacking is the most reliable trigger mechanism. The format is:

> After [existing habit], I will [new habit].

Examples:
- After I pour my morning coffee, I will write three sentences.
- After I sit down at my desk, I will do five minutes of stretching.
- After I brush my teeth at night, I will read one page.

The existing habit acts as a reliable cue. You don't need to remember — the trigger fires automatically.

### 3. Track it visibly

A simple checkbox for each day is enough. The act of ticking a box creates a small reward signal. Missing a day becomes visible, which creates mild discomfort — a useful motivator.

The rule: never miss twice. One missed day is an accident. Two missed days is the start of a new habit — the habit of not doing it.

## How long does it actually take?

The popular claim is 21 days. The actual research (Lally et al., 2010) found it takes between 18 and 254 days, with an average of 66 days. The range is wide because it depends on the complexity of the behaviour and the individual.

The practical takeaway: don't expect automaticity in three weeks. Give it two to three months before judging whether a habit has formed.

## What to track

Track the behaviour, not the outcome. "Did I run today?" not "Did I lose weight this week?" Outcomes lag behind behaviour by weeks or months. Tracking the behaviour keeps you focused on what you can control.

---

The system is simple: start small, attach to a trigger, track daily. The difficulty is not in understanding it — it's in not overcomplicating it when motivation is high and you want to do more.

Do less than you think you should. Track it. Repeat.
