Exercise is the habit most people want and most people fail to maintain. Gym memberships spike in January and return to baseline by March. The problem is almost never motivation — it's setup.

Here's what actually determines whether an exercise habit sticks.

## The setup mistakes that kill exercise habits

### Starting too hard

The most common mistake: starting with a programme that's too demanding. Five days a week, one hour per session, high intensity. This works for two weeks, then life intervenes, you miss a few days, and the streak is broken.

The fix: start with a frequency and duration you could maintain even on your worst week. Two sessions of 20 minutes is a better starting point than five sessions of 60 minutes. You can always add more. You can't undo a broken habit.

### No fixed time

"I'll exercise when I have time" means you won't exercise. Time doesn't appear — it gets allocated.

Pick a specific time slot and treat it as a non-negotiable appointment. Morning works best for most people because it's before the day's interruptions accumulate. But the best time is the one you'll actually use.

### Choosing exercise you hate

Discipline can sustain an unpleasant habit for a few weeks. It can't sustain it for years. If you hate running, don't build a running habit. Find a form of movement you're at least neutral about.

The options are wider than most people consider: walking, cycling, swimming, climbing, martial arts, team sports, yoga, weightlifting, rowing. The physiological benefits are broadly similar across types. The habit durability depends almost entirely on whether you'll keep doing it.

## The minimum viable exercise habit

The research on exercise benefits shows significant returns from surprisingly small amounts of movement:

- **150 minutes per week** of moderate activity (brisk walking) covers the baseline health recommendations
- **75 minutes per week** of vigorous activity achieves similar results
- Even **10 minutes per day** of movement produces measurable improvements in mood and cognitive function

The minimum viable habit: 10 minutes of movement, every day, at the same time. That's it. Not a workout programme — a daily movement habit.

Once the habit is established (you do it automatically, without debate), increase the duration or intensity. But the habit comes first.

## Reducing friction

The biggest predictor of exercise habit success is how easy it is to start. Every barrier between you and beginning the exercise is a potential exit point.

Practical friction reductions:
- Sleep in your gym clothes or lay them out the night before
- Keep equipment visible — a kettlebell in the corner of the room, a yoga mat unrolled
- Choose a gym that's on your commute route, not out of the way
- Have a default workout you can do without thinking — removes the decision cost
- Track completion immediately after finishing

## Handling missed days

Missing one day is not a problem. Missing two days in a row is the start of a new pattern.

The rule: if you miss a day, the next day is non-negotiable. No exceptions, no rescheduling. The session might be shorter than usual — that's fine. The point is re-establishing the pattern.

Don't try to make up missed sessions. A missed Monday doesn't mean doing double on Tuesday. It means doing Tuesday's session as planned.

## Tracking exercise habits

Track completion, not performance. Did you exercise today? Yes or no.

Performance metrics (distance, weight lifted, pace) are useful for training programmes, but they're the wrong thing to track when building the habit. They introduce too many variables and create opportunities to feel like you failed even when you showed up.

Once the habit is solid — you exercise automatically, without internal debate — you can layer performance tracking on top.

---

The exercise habit is built the same way as any other habit: start smaller than feels necessary, attach it to a fixed trigger, reduce friction, and track consistently. The fitness results follow from the habit. Build the habit first.
