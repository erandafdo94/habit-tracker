The four laws of behaviour change are the operating system underneath *Atomic Habits*. If you strip out the anecdotes and the padding, what's left is a four-step loop: **cue, craving, response, reward**, and a law that makes each step easier for habits you want, harder for habits you don't.

Most summaries list the laws and move on. The laws are only useful if you know how to apply each one to a specific habit. This post is that.

## The four laws, in one line each

1. **Make it obvious.** (Cue)
2. **Make it attractive.** (Craving)
3. **Make it easy.** (Response)
4. **Make it satisfying.** (Reward)

Invert all four to break a habit:

1. Make it invisible.
2. Make it unattractive.
3. Make it difficult.
4. Make it unsatisfying.

That's the whole framework. The power isn't in knowing the laws, it's in running every habit decision through them.

## 1. Make it obvious, design your cues

Habits don't start in the brain. They start in the **environment**. Your brain spots a cue, associates it with a reward, and fires the routine.

Practical moves:

- **Implementation intentions.** Don't say "I'll meditate more." Say: *"I will meditate for 10 minutes at 7am in the living room."* Time + location nails the cue.
- **Habit stacking.** Anchor the new habit to an existing one: *"After I pour my morning coffee, I'll write for 10 minutes."* The existing habit becomes the cue.
- **Environment design.** Want to read more? Put a book on the pillow. Want to stop scrolling? Charge the phone in another room. Make the cues for good habits louder and cues for bad habits invisible.

## 2. Make it attractive, engineer the craving

A habit runs only if the brain **expects** a reward. That expectation, the dopamine spike before the action, is the craving. The habit isn't driven by the reward itself; it's driven by the anticipation.

Moves:

- **Temptation bundling.** Pair something you need to do with something you want to do. "I only watch Netflix while on the exercise bike." The wanted thing pulls the needed thing behind it.
- **Join a culture where your desired behaviour is normal.** Identity is contagious. Pick the tribe where the habit is the baseline, and you'll inherit the cravings.
- **Reframe the story.** "I *get* to train" instead of "I *have* to train." The language runs the dopamine.

## 3. Make it easy, reduce the friction

Motivation is overrated. Friction is underrated. Almost every habit is a story about how many steps stand between you and the action.

Moves:

- **The two-minute rule.** Scale the habit down until it takes two minutes or less. "Read before bed" becomes "read one page." Most of the value of a habit is in showing up; the two-minute version guarantees you show up.
- **Reduce friction for good habits.** Lay out tomorrow's gym clothes tonight. Pre-chop vegetables. Pre-open the doc you need to write in.
- **Increase friction for bad habits.** Log out of the app. Delete the bookmark. Put the junk food on the top shelf. Every extra step is a chance for the craving to lose.

## 4. Make it satisfying, close the loop fast

The brain prioritises **immediate** rewards. Habits that only pay off months later, exercise, saving, learning, fight an uphill battle against habits that pay off right now. The fix is to manufacture an immediate reward.

Moves:

- **Track the habit visibly.** Crossing a day off a chart is a small but immediate reward. (A [habit tracker](/) externalises this reward.)
- **Never miss twice.** One skip is noise. Two skips is a new habit forming. The rule that matters is showing up the day after a miss.
- **Pair the habit with a small, immediate reward.** After the run, the shower; after the writing session, the coffee. Keep the pairing consistent.

## How to run a habit through all four

Pick a habit you want to install. Write the habit at the top. Under it, write four lines:

- How can I make it more **obvious**?
- How can I make it more **attractive**?
- How can I make it **easier**?
- How can I make it **more satisfying**?

If you can't answer all four, the habit isn't set up to succeed. Fix the weak law before you start.

The laws aren't magic. They just name the four levers that actually move behaviour. Most habit failures are a law that wasn't applied. Apply all four, and most habits hold.
