The hardest part of a new habit is remembering to do it. Habit stacking solves this by attaching the new behaviour to one you already run on autopilot.

The format:

> After [current habit], I will [new habit].

The existing habit is the cue. You don't need willpower or reminders — the trigger fires because the anchor is already automatic. It's one of the most tactical moves inside the four laws of behaviour change, specifically the first law: *make it obvious.* ([Full walkthrough of the four laws.](/blog/4-laws-of-behavior-change))

## Why it works

Habits are stored as sequences. When one behaviour fires, the next in the sequence is primed. It's why you can drive a familiar route on autopilot, or why brushing your teeth automatically makes you think about flossing.

Habit stacking exploits this deliberately. By anchoring a new behaviour to an existing sequence, you borrow the automaticity of the anchor.

## How to build a habit stack

### Step 1: List your existing anchors

Write down the things you do every day without thinking:

- Wake up
- Make coffee or tea
- Sit down at your desk
- Eat breakfast or lunch
- Brush teeth (morning and evening)
- Get into bed

These are your anchors. Each one is a potential trigger.

### Step 2: Match the new habit to the right anchor

The anchor and the new habit should be in the same context. Don't stack "do 10 push-ups" onto "sit down at my desk" — the context is wrong. Stack it onto "get out of bed" or "finish my morning coffee."

Mismatched context is one of the main reasons stacks fail.

### Step 3: Be specific

Vague stacks don't work. "After breakfast, I'll exercise" is too vague. "After I put my breakfast plate in the sink, I will do 10 push-ups in the kitchen" is specific enough to actually happen.

The more precisely you define the trigger and the behaviour, the more reliably the stack fires.

## Examples by goal

### Health

- After I pour my morning coffee, I will take my vitamins.
- After I sit down for lunch, I will drink a full glass of water.
- After I get into bed, I will do five minutes of stretching.

### Focus and work

- After I open my laptop, I will write my three priorities for the day before opening email.
- After I finish a meeting, I will write one action item before moving on.
- After I close my laptop at the end of the day, I will write tomorrow's first task.

### Learning

- After I sit on the train, I will open my book instead of my phone.
- After dinner, I will spend 10 minutes on a language app.
- After I make my evening tea, I will read one article.

### Identity

- After I wake up, I will write one thing I'm focused on today before checking my phone.
- After I get into bed, I will think of one thing that went well today.

Each stack is a vote for who you're becoming. ([More on identity-based habits here.](/blog/identity-based-habits))

## Stacking multiple habits

You can chain habits into a sequence:

> Wake up → make coffee → take vitamins → sit down → write three priorities → open email

Each step triggers the next. The chain becomes a single routine that runs automatically.

Keep chains short — three to four habits max. Long chains break when any single link fails. Once the chain is solid, you can extend it.

## When habit stacks fail

**The anchor isn't consistent enough.** If your morning routine varies significantly day to day, it's a weak anchor. Use the most consistent behaviours you have.

**The new habit is too long.** If it takes more than five minutes, it disrupts the flow of the anchor routine. Start with something that takes under two minutes.

**The context doesn't match.** Stacking a physical habit onto a sedentary anchor, or a quiet habit onto a noisy one, creates friction. Match the environment.

---

Habit stacking isn't magic. It's a way of reducing the cognitive load of remembering to do something new. The habit still has to be done — the stack just makes sure you don't forget.

Pick one anchor. Pick one new habit. Write the stack down. Do it tomorrow.
