Morning routines have become an industry. Cold plunges, journaling, meditation, exercise, reading, gratitude lists, the ideal morning routine apparently takes four hours and requires waking up at 4am.

Most of it is noise. Here's what's actually worth doing.

## Why mornings matter for habits

The morning is the highest-willpower window for most people. Decision fatigue hasn't set in. Interruptions are minimal. The day hasn't gone sideways yet.

Habits placed in the morning have a higher completion rate than habits placed later in the day. This isn't a rule, night owls and shift workers have different windows, but for most people, morning is the most reliable slot.

The morning is also a natural chain of anchors, wake up → coffee → shower, which makes it the easiest place to install a new habit via stacking. ([Full habit stacking guide here.](/blog/habit-stacking-guide))

## What's worth building

### Exercise (any kind, any duration)

The research on exercise is unambiguous. It improves mood, cognition, sleep, and long-term health. Even 10 minutes of movement in the morning has measurable effects on focus and energy.

The barrier isn't knowledge. Everyone knows exercise is good. The barrier is friction. Reduce it: sleep in your gym clothes, keep equipment visible, commit to a minimum of five minutes. ([Full exercise habit guide.](/blog/exercise-habit-guide))

### A fixed start ritual

A short, consistent sequence that signals "the day is starting" helps the brain shift into focus mode. Could be making coffee, five minutes of stretching, writing three sentences. The content matters less than the consistency.

The ritual acts as a cue for everything that follows. This is the first law of behaviour change, *make it obvious*, in action. ([Full walkthrough of the four laws.](/blog/4-laws-of-behavior-change))

### No phone for the first 30 minutes

Starting the day by reading notifications puts you in reactive mode immediately. You're responding to other people's priorities before you've addressed your own.

This is one of the highest-leverage changes most people can make. It's also one of the hardest, because the phone is usually the first thing people reach for. The fix is environmental: put it in another room overnight. Friction beats willpower.

## What's overrated

### Journaling (for most people)

Journaling works for people who think by writing. For everyone else, it's a chore that produces guilt when skipped. If you don't enjoy it, don't force it. A one-line identity log is more useful and takes 20 seconds. ([More on identity-based habits.](/blog/identity-based-habits))

### Meditation (as commonly taught)

Formal meditation has real benefits, but the 20-minute silent sitting practice doesn't work for most people long-term. If you want the benefits of mindfulness, a five-minute breathing exercise or a short walk without headphones gets similar results with less friction.

### Cold showers

The evidence for cold exposure is real but modest. The main benefit most people report is psychological, the sense of having done something hard first thing. If that works for you, keep it. If it's a thing you feel you should do, skip it.

## How to design a morning routine

1. **Identify your non-negotiables.** What are the two or three things that, if done before noon, make the day feel successful? Start there.
2. **Sequence them after an existing anchor.** Wake up → coffee → exercise → shower is a chain. Each step triggers the next.
3. **Set a minimum version.** On bad days, what's the shortest version of the routine you'll still do? Five minutes of movement and no phone for 20 minutes is enough to count.
4. **Track it.** A simple checkbox for each habit. The streak creates accountability, but use it carefully. ([When streaks help and when they backfire.](/blog/habit-streaks-psychology))

## The one that carries the others

If you can only install one morning habit, pick the one that makes the rest easier. Exercise does this for most people, it shifts mood, sleep, and energy in ways that make every other habit more likely to stick. Single habits that move many others are called keystone habits. ([Full breakdown of keystone habits.](/blog/keystone-habits-that-change-everything))

## The real goal

A morning routine is not a performance. It's not something to optimise endlessly or compare to what successful people do. The goal is to start the day in a way that makes the rest of it easier.

That looks different for everyone. Find the two or three things that actually help *you*, do them consistently, and ignore the rest.
